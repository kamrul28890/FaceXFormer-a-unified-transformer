\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Task-specific facial analysis.}
Facial analysis has traditionally been decomposed into separate prediction problems, each with its own architecture, preprocessing pipeline, loss function, and evaluation protocol. Landmark localization methods such as Wing Loss~\cite{wing2018} and STAR Loss~\cite{star2023} focus on spatially precise point regression and explicitly address annotation ambiguity. Head-pose methods such as TokenHPE~\cite{tokenhpe2023} formulate pose estimation around orientation-aware transformer tokens, while large-scale recognition methods such as ArcFace~\cite{arcface2019} and PartialFC~\cite{partialfc2021} optimize highly discriminative identity embeddings under large-class classification. These task-specialized systems remain strong baselines because they encode the output structure and supervision signal of a single task very directly. However, they also illustrate the central limitation motivating FaceXFormer: a practical face-analysis pipeline often needs segmentation, landmarks, pose, attributes, demographics, expression, visibility, and recognition simultaneously, and running many independent backbones duplicates computation and introduces incompatible preprocessing assumptions.

\paragraph{Multi-task face-analysis models.}
Earlier multi-task face models demonstrated that facial tasks can share useful representations. HyperFace~\cite{hyperface2019} jointly modeled face detection, landmark localization, pose estimation, and gender recognition by fusing intermediate CNN features, while All-in-One CNN~\cite{allinone2017} expanded the CNN multi-task setting to detection, alignment, pose, gender, smile, age, and recognition. These models established the value of task synergy, but their convolutional designs predate the task-token and transformer-decoder paradigm used by recent generalist models. SwinFace~\cite{swinface2024} moves toward transformer-based multi-task face analysis with a shared Swin backbone and task-specific subnets for face recognition, expression recognition, age estimation, and attribute estimation. QFace~\cite{qface2024} further introduces task-adaptive query vectors and cross-attention over fused multi-stage features. Faceptor~\cite{faceptor2024} is the closest predecessor to FaceXFormer: it uses a single encoder with task-specific queries and standardized output heads, and is trained over multiple face-perception datasets. Nevertheless, these systems still cover fewer tasks than FaceXFormer and generally use heavier decoder structures. FaceXFormer is therefore positioned not merely as another multi-task face model, but as an attempt to unify a wider set of heterogeneous outputs under a lightweight real-time transformer design~\cite{facexformer2025}.

\paragraph{Tokenized and unified transformer architectures.}
The architectural design of FaceXFormer is part of a broader shift from task-specific CNNs to tokenized transformer systems. DETR~\cite{detr2020} showed that learned queries can serve as output slots for set prediction, motivating later task-query and task-token formulations. Swin Transformer~\cite{swin2021} provides a hierarchical vision backbone whose shifted-window attention is well suited to dense and global visual prediction, and SegFormer~\cite{segformer2021} showed that multi-scale transformer features can be fused efficiently with a lightweight MLP decoder. In face analysis, FaRL~\cite{farl2022} demonstrated that large-scale visual-linguistic pretraining can produce general facial representations transferable to downstream tasks. FaceXFormer combines several of these ideas: a hierarchical Swin-B encoder, MLP-based multi-scale fusion, learnable task tokens, and a lightweight FaceX decoder. Its distinctive claim is the use of bidirectional task--face exchange: task tokens query face features, and face features are refined by task-token feedback. This mechanism is intended to make a shallow two-block decoder competitive with deeper transformer decoders while preserving real-time inference.

\paragraph{Reproducibility and implementation completeness.}
Our work also belongs to the literature on reproducibility in empirical machine learning. Pineau et al.~\cite{pineau2021} argue that code availability, dataset details, hyperparameters, evaluation protocols, and compute descriptions are essential for reliable reproduction. Lipton and Steinhardt~\cite{lipton2018} similarly criticize empirical ML papers when gains are difficult to attribute to clearly specified mechanisms rather than hidden implementation choices. This issue is especially acute for FaceXFormer because the reported system depends on many coupled details: task-specific datasets, preprocessing, token counts, loss definitions, task-balanced sampling, staged optimization, and loss weighting. The original paper verifies strong performance and efficiency, but the public release lacks the complete training loop, dataloaders, loss implementations, and sampler needed to reproduce those claims directly. Our project therefore treats reproduction as both an engineering task and an empirical test of the architectural claims: whether the reported efficiency and multi-task performance persist when the missing training infrastructure is reconstructed from the paper, code, and explicit assumptions.

\paragraph{Position of this reproduction.}
In summary, prior work establishes three threads: strong task-specific models, increasingly capable multi-task face systems, and general transformer-based architectures. FaceXFormer sits at the intersection of these threads by attempting ten-task unification with a compact decoder. Our reproduction is complementary rather than competitive: instead of proposing a new architecture, we evaluate the reproducibility of FaceXFormer's claims by rebuilding the omitted training components, documenting paper--code gaps, and measuring which implementation choices materially affect the final results.
